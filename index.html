Chernobyl: From Construction to Catastrophe and Beyond

Chernobyl: Dalla Costruzione alla Catastrofe e Oltre

1. The Beginning: Planning and Construction (1960s‚Äì1983)

  • 1967: Planning begins for the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in northern Ukraine.
  • 1970: Construction begins near the new city of Pripyat.
  • 1977‚Äì1983: Four RBMK-1000 reactors become operational:
    • 1977: Reactor 1
    • 1978: Reactor 2
    • 1981: Reactor 3
    • 1983: Reactor 4 (the one that would later explode)
  • Ignored design flaws:
    • Positive void coefficient
    • Graphite-tipped control rods
    • No containment structure

2. Prelude to Disaster: April 1986

Objective: Test whether turbines could power coolant pumps during a blackout.

  • April 25: Morning: power reduction begins
  • Afternoon: Delayed 9 hours by Kyiv grid demand
  • Evening: Operators exhausted, new shift unprepared

3. The Disaster: April 26, 1986, 1:23 AM

  • Reactor power dropped to 30 MW (too low)
  • Operators disabled safety systems
  • Withdrew too many control rods
  • Operated the reactor in unstable conditions
  • 1:23:04 AM: Test begins ‚Üí coolant flow decreases ‚Üí steam increases reactivity
  • AZ-5 button pressed: Graphite rod tips temporarily increase reactivity
  • 1:23:40 AM: Explosion ‚Üí 1,000-ton lid blown off ‚Üí reactor core exposed

4. Immediate Aftermath (April‚ÄìMay 1986)

  • First Responders: Unaware of radiation, used water to fight fires; many died within weeks
  • Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS): Vomiting, burns, unconsciousness; 28 deaths by May, 134 diagnosed
  • Evacuation: April 27: Pripyat evacuated (49,000 people); April 28: Sweden detects radiation, USSR admits accident

5. Global Fallout and Soviet Secrecy

Radioactive cloud spread across Europe ‚Äî key isotopes released: Iodine-131, Cesium-137, Strontium-90. USSR proceeded with May Day parades despite risks.

6. Liquidators and the Sarcophagus

Over 600,000 liquidators cleared debris manually and built the sarcophagus, completed November 1986. Longterm death estimates: 4,000‚Äì90,000+.

7. From Recovery to Shutdown (1987‚Äì2000)

  • 1987: Reactors 1, 2, and 3 restarted
  • 1991: Reactor 2 fire; closed permanently
  • 1996: Reactor 1 closed
  • 2000: Reactor 3 shut down, ending operations

8. New Safe Confinement (2000‚Äì2016)

Original sarcophagus deteriorating; NSC built and completed in 2016‚Äîa steel arch structure for remote dismantling, designed to last 100 years.

9. Legacy and Current Status (2025)

  • Health effects: Spike in thyroid cancer; WHO: 4,000‚Äì9,000 deaths; Greenpeace: tens of thousands
  • Exclusion Zone: 30¬†km radius around plant; wildlife thriving; some residents returned (‚Äúself-settlers‚Äù)
  • Tourism: Guided tours to Pripyat and reactor site

  • Political consequences: USSR credibility eroded; contributed to collapse
  • Cultural influence: Documentaries, HBO‚Äôs Chernobyl; books, films, video games

üî¨ What Is Nuclear Energy?

  • Fission ‚Äì splitting large atoms into smaller ones (used today)
  • Fusion ‚Äì merging small atoms into larger ones (future of nuclear energy)

Nuclear energy produces massive amounts of electricity with no carbon emissions during operation...

‚ò¢Ô∏è 1. Fission: The Power Source of Today‚Äôs Nuclear Plants

Fission happens when a heavy atom (like uranium-235 or plutonium-239) splits into smaller parts. This reaction releases a huge amount of heat and free neutrons, causing a chain reaction controlled to generate electricity.

‚öôÔ∏è How a Nuclear Power Plant Works

  1. Fuel rods with enriched uranium or plutonium are placed in the reactor core.
  2. Controlled fission reactions produce heat.
  3. Heat turns water into steam.
  4. Steam drives turbines ‚Üí generators make electricity.
  5. Control rods absorb excess neutrons to control the reaction.
  6. Coolants absorb and transfer heat.
  7. The entire system is protected by a containment structure.

üõ°Ô∏è Safety Features in Modern Reactors

  • Negative feedback loops slow the reaction if it overheats
  • Multiple backup cooling systems
  • Containment domes prevent radioactive leaks
  • Passive safety systems that work even without power or human input

üö® 2. What Went Wrong at Chernobyl

At Chernobyl, a flawed RBMK design and human errors led to a runaway reaction: positive void coefficient, graphite-tipped control rods, no containment building, and an AZ-5 shutdown spike.

üåû 3. Fusion: The Future of Nuclear Energy

Fusion combines light atoms (like deuterium and tritium) into heavier ones, releasing enormous energy‚Äîthe same process as in the sun.

üåç 4. Nuclear Energy in the Real World

Over 30 countries use nuclear energy, providing ~10% of global electricity, clean and reliable baseload power.
